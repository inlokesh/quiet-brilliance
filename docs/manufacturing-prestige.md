# The Machine That Manufactures Prestige

A LinkedIn post recently celebrated Gopakumar, who topped the IIT-JEE in 1987 with All India Rank 1. The post was warm and genuinely admiring. Gopakumar chose Physics over Computer Science when every incentive pointed elsewhere. He did his PhD at Princeton under Nobel Laureate David Gross, did a postdoc at Harvard, and then came back to India. Today he directs the International Centre for Theoretical Sciences in Bengaluru. The post held him up, rightly, as someone with a different disposition — someone who resisted the gravitational pull of the H1B queue and chose fundamental science and his own country instead.

The admiration is deserved. But in the comments, an IIT graduate named Chandra H. said something that stopped me.

“Indian education including IIT is sh1t. It’s overly theoretical. And produces mostly nothing path-breaking and he is no exception. Indians are too easily impressed by exam ranks. It’s sad.”

When others pushed back, Chandra didn’t retreat. He went deeper.

“Real scientists who made physics did so via observation and experimentation, not just memorizing and calculating. In India, there is not much observation and not much experimentation. Only memory, marks, gatekeeping and unearned privileges. India is the only country where people are respected by their exam ranks, not actual value creation.”

And then, most sharply of all: IIT education “offers no context but manufactures prestige.”

I read that and felt something loosen in my chest. Because I have been carrying a version of this for over thirty years without the language for it.

-----

In 1991, I began preparing for JEE 93. I was in Bangalore. I was not as disciplined as I should have been, and I knew it even then, though I couldn’t explain why the discipline wouldn’t come.

What I had instead was appetite. I bought physics and mathematics books from Navakarnataka Publications — Mir Publishers books, originally written in English, hardbound, printed on paper of a quality that made the Eastern Economy Editions of Resnick and Halliday feel thin by comparison. They were also priced cheaply enough that a schoolboy could afford them. I know this because I skipped lunch to save the money to buy them. Not as an act of strategic self-discipline, but because I wanted those books more than I wanted to eat.

I was enrolled in Brilliant Tutorials. I was, by any reasonable measure, doing the things a serious JEE aspirant was supposed to do.

But there was something wrong with my relationship to the exam itself. When I sat down to study for the pleasure of understanding, something opened up. When I sat down to study for the exam, something closed. I could not bridge that gap. I did not know then that this gap had a name, or that it was not a personal failing but a structural problem — the structure of the system itself.

Around the same time, in April 1992, my mother sent me to Sunday school. The reasons were practical: she wanted me to improve my English. The teacher threw in a carrot — he would also teach me chemistry. He was doing his PhD in Organic Chemistry at the Indian Institute of Science.

So there were two distinct things happening. On Sunday afternoons, he taught the Sunday school curriculum — scripture, faith, formation. Three evenings a week, I would walk to his hostel room at IISc, and there, in that small personal space, he would teach me organic chemistry for JEE.

I want to sit with that image for a moment. A young PhD student giving his own time, in his own room, to a boy whose mother had originally just wanted him to learn English. No rank. No gate. No exam at the end of the relationship. Just a scientist and a curious student and the strange, tangible world of molecules that have shapes and reactions that have smells.

That, almost by accident, was the kind of education Chandra is describing. Contextual. Grounded. Connected to a real human being doing real science.

In November 1992, I embraced the Christian faith. And this introduced a new confusion into an already complicated relationship with education. The culture I had grown up in treated the IIT dream as something close to sacred — rank as proxy for intelligence, intelligence as proxy for worth, worth as the thing your family could finally exhale about. My faith told me this hierarchy was false. That exam rank was not the highest good. That there was a different accounting entirely.

I went into JEE 93 carrying all of this. The Soviet books. The Sunday school chemistry. The Reformed faith that had quietly dismantled the temple I was supposed to be worshipping at. I did not do well. I could not.

-----

Chandra’s critique is not simply that Indian education is bad at producing scientists. It is more precise than that. He is saying that the Brahminic model of learning — built on transmission, preservation, memorization, gatekeeping — is structurally incapable of producing discovery. Discovery requires a feedback loop with reality. It requires observation, experiment, failure, revision. It requires that the people doing the learning are not insulated from the consequences of being wrong.

The IIT-JEE selects for something real — it selects for people who can perform under extreme pressure on extremely difficult problems. That is not nothing. Gopakumar is not nothing. But the system that produced him also produced a culture in which his greatest distinction, in the eyes of many who celebrated that LinkedIn post, is still his rank from 1987. AIR 1. Thirty-seven years later, that number leads the story.

Chandra pressed the point further when someone pushed back: IIT education “offers no context but manufactures prestige.” The prestige circulates. The context — the ecosystem of real problems, failed experiments, productive rivalries, engagement with the physical world — is largely absent.

What the system cannot account for is someone who loves learning but cannot perform on its terms. Someone who reads Mir Publishers books for the joy of it and freezes when the exam begins. Someone accidentally educated in a Sunday school by an IISc chemist who had no incentive to teach for marks.

The system has no category for that person. It simply ejects them.

-----

I am not writing this to relitigate my JEE result. I am writing this because Chandra H., an IIT graduate with no reason to flatter anyone, looked at a post celebrating AIR 1 from 1987 and said: the ceiling this system imposes applies even to him. The system manufactures prestige. It does not, by its nature, manufacture the conditions for genuine discovery.

That is a harder claim than it sounds. It is not an attack on Gopakumar, who by all accounts has done serious work and made a serious choice in returning to India. It is an indictment of what we collectively worship — and what we have built our institutions to produce.

Rank. Memory. Gatekeeping. Manufactured prestige.

The real scientists, Chandra reminds us, worked differently. They observed. They experimented. They were embedded in ecosystems where reality pushed back.

I learned a little of that, once, in a Sunday school classroom in Bangalore, from a man teaching organic chemistry for no marks at all.

I wish I had known then that this was the more valuable education. I wish the system had had room for it.
